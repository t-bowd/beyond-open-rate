## Variant A

You're about to spend $8,000 fixing your email program.

What gets fixed first?

Most brands start with the welcome flow. Makes sense — it's the most visible, it touches every new customer, and it feels like the obvious win.

We start with whatever has the highest dollar value per hour of work.

Usually that's authentication. SPF, DKIM, DMARC properly configured can take 90 minutes and shift deliverability 15–20 percentage points. If you're sending 200,000 emails a month, that's 30,000–40,000 more emails landing in the primary inbox. Immediate revenue lift for less than two hours of work.

Then deliverability cleanup. List hygiene, suppression rules, engagement-based segmentation. Another few hours. Another meaningful shift in what percentage of your list actually sees your emails.

Then we look at flows — not by what feels important, but by where the biggest leak is.

Sometimes that's welcome. Often it's not.

Browse abandonment flows typically have worse coverage than cart abandonment but represent 3–5x the volume of events. Post-purchase flows in consumable categories can drive 18–25% of total repeat purchase revenue but half the brands we audit don't have one running.

Priority is dollar value divided by effort required.

Not what feels most visible. Not what you think you should do. Just math.

If the work doesn't pay for itself in the first 60 days, it doesn't get prioritised.

## Variant B

We onboarded a brand last month doing $4M a year.

They wanted to rebuild their welcome series. Fair enough — it was three emails, pretty generic, clearly hadn't been touched in two years.

We ran the numbers.

Their welcome flow was converting at 4.2%. Not great, but not a disaster. Fixing it might get them to 6–7%. Maybe an extra $3,000 a month if we're optimistic.

Their DMARC wasn't configured. 31% of their emails were landing in promotions or spam. Fixing that would take two hours and shift maybe $18,000 a month back into the primary inbox.

So we fixed authentication first.

This is the part that frustrates people. Everyone wants to start with the thing that feels like a project. The welcome series. The win-back campaign. The new subscriber pop-up.

We start with whatever has the highest return per hour of effort.

Authentication and deliverability almost always come first. The math is just better. You're not optimising 3–5% of your list, you're fixing the infrastructure that affects 100% of it.

After that, we prioritise by leak size. Not by what flows exist, but by what revenue is walking out the door that shouldn't be.

Sometimes that's welcome. Often it's browse abandonment, post-purchase, or replenishment flows that either don't exist or are badly broken.

The methodology isn't complicated.

Revenue opportunity divided by hours required. Highest number wins.
