## Variant A

You paid $30 to acquire that customer.

They bought once. Maybe twice.

Then silence.

Most brands wait 90 days before sending a win-back email. By then, that customer has already bought from a competitor. Your window closed weeks ago.

The best-performing win-back flows trigger at 30–45 days. Not 90.

Here's why: at 30 days, the customer still remembers your brand. They're receptive. The relationship isn't cold yet.

At 90 days, you're a forgotten cart notification in a sea of other brands.

And when that win-back finally lands, it's usually just a discount. No context. No reason to care.

The brands that get this right don't lead with 15% off. They lead with something new. A product the customer hasn't seen. A restock they were waiting for. A reason that isn't desperation.

If you've got a win-back flow, check the trigger. If it's set to 90 days, you're leaving 60 days of buyable intent on the table.

## Variant B

Your win-back flow isn't broken.

It's just late.

Most e-commerce brands have one running. It's in the system. It ticks along. They consider it handled.

The problem is the trigger.

If it's set to fire at 90 days of inactivity, you've already lost. That customer bought somewhere else at day 40. Or day 50. They didn't wait for you.

The best window is 30–45 days. Early enough that you're still top of mind. Late enough that you're not annoying them.

And the email itself can't just be a discount. That signals weakness. It tells the customer you only want them back because you're desperate.

The win-back emails that work give a genuine reason to return. New stock. A product they browsed but didn't buy. Something that feels like timing, not begging.

If your win-back is set to 90 days, you've got the right asset in the wrong position. It's doing nothing.
