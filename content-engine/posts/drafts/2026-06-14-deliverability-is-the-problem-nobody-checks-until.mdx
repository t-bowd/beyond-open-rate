## Variant A

Your open rate is 22%.

You've been hovering there for six months. Stable. Fine. Industry average, maybe a bit better.

So email's working.

Except 18% of your list isn't receiving your emails at all.

They're going to spam. Or being rejected by the server before they even land. Your ESP doesn't show you those people. They're still on your list. Still counted in your sends. Just invisible.

The math: if 18% aren't receiving emails, your real open rate among people who actually got the email isn't 22%. It's 27%.

Sounds good, except you're haemorrhaging reach and you didn't know.

Deliverability problems don't announce themselves. Your dashboard doesn't flash red. Opens stay stable because the people not getting your emails… aren't opening them. Obvious when you say it out loud.

By the time you notice, you've been slowly losing access to the list you paid to build.

Most brands check deliverability after a campaign bombs. The smart ones check it quarterly, minimum, whether anything looks wrong or not.

Your 22% might actually be fine. Or it might be hiding a 15% delivery bleed you won't see until Q4 revenue misses forecast.

## Variant B

You're only seeing the people who received your email.

That's the bit that breaks most founders' brains when they finally look at deliverability.

Your ESP reports on successful deliveries. Opens, clicks, revenue. All calculated from the emails that landed.

The emails that bounced to spam, got blocked by the recipient server, or were rejected outright? Not in the report.

It's survivorship bias in your dashboard.

If 100 emails go out and 20 land in spam, your system shows 80 sends. 18 opens. That's 22.5%. Looks fine.

But the real open rate — among everyone you tried to reach — is 18%. And you'll never know unless you specifically go looking.

Here's the worse part: deliverability degrades slowly. A few percentage points a quarter. Your open rate stays roughly flat because the calculation keeps changing. Fewer emails delivered, fewer opens, same ratio.

You don't notice until a major provider changes their filtering rules, or you hit a spam trap you didn't know was on your list, and suddenly 40% of your sends don't land.

If your open rate has been "stable" for more than three months, that's not necessarily a good sign.
