## Variant A

You're spending 4 hours a week writing a newsletter.

It gets a 19% open rate. Maybe 2% click. You're tracking the metrics. You feel productive.

But your repeat purchase rate hasn't moved in 6 months.

That's because a weekly broadcast isn't retention. It's interruption.

Retention happens when the message is triggered by what the customer just did. Not what day of the week it is.

They bought once 45 days ago. Radio silence. Then your newsletter hits talking about a product category they've never shown interest in.

They browsed winter coats. Left the site. Your newsletter arrives three days later with a skincare promotion.

You're not staying front of mind. You're training them to ignore you.

Real retention is automated sequences that respond to behaviour:

Someone buys for the first time — they get onboarding that builds habit and increases likelihood of purchase two.

Someone hits 60 days since last order — they get a winback sequence, not your generic weekly blast.

Someone browses a category — they get follow-up on that specific interest, not whatever you decided to write about this Tuesday.

Your newsletter can exist. But if it's your retention strategy, you don't have a retention strategy.

You have a content calendar.

## Variant B

Your list has three types of people on it right now:

Someone who bought 6 days ago.
Someone who bought 6 months ago.
Someone who's never bought.

You just sent all three the same email.

That's not a retention strategy. That's lazy broadcasting.

The person who bought 6 days ago doesn't need a discount. They need education on how to get more value from what they just purchased.

The person who bought 6 months ago doesn't need your brand story. They need a reason to come back that's specific to what they bought last time.

The person who's never bought doesn't care about your new product launch. They need social proof and a lower barrier to first purchase.

One message. Three completely different needs.

And you wonder why your repeat purchase rate is stuck.

Retention isn't about staying top of mind with volume. It's about sending the right message at the moment it's actually relevant.

That requires automation. Segmentation. Triggers based on behaviour, not your publishing schedule.

A weekly newsletter can work as part of the mix. But if it's the only thing you're doing, you're not doing retention.

You're doing the same thing to everyone and hoping some of it sticks.
