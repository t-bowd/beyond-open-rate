Your list always contains people who've stopped engaging. They signed up, opened a few emails, then disappeared. After three months of silence, they're officially **lapsed subscribers** — and they're costing you money and damaging your sender reputation.

A **win-back email campaign** is your last systematic attempt to re-engage them before removing them from your list entirely. Done well, you'll recover 10–15% of lapsed subscribers. Done poorly, you'll accelerate their journey to the spam folder.

## What qualifies as 'lapsed'

Most programs define lapsed as 90–180 days without an open or click. The exact threshold depends on your send frequency.

If you send three times a week, 90 days means someone ignored roughly 40 emails. That's lapsed. If you send monthly, 180 days (six missed emails) is more appropriate. The specific number matters less than consistency — pick a threshold, document it, and apply it across your entire list.

Track engagement by actual behaviour, not just opens. Since Apple's Mail Privacy Protection renders open rates unreliable, prioritise clicks, purchases, or other conversion events. Someone who hasn't clicked anything in six months is functionally dormant regardless of what their open rate says.

## Why bother trying to win them back

Two reasons: economics and deliverability.

First, acquisition costs. If you paid $8–15 to acquire each subscriber through ads or content, letting them die without a fight means throwing that investment away. Winning back even 12% of a 10,000-person lapsed segment recovers 1,200 subscribers you've already paid for.

Second, deliverability damage. Mailbox providers track engagement at the individual level. If someone consistently ignores your emails, Gmail and Outlook interpret that as "this sender isn't relevant to this person." Keep sending to unresponsive addresses and you train the algorithms that your mail is unwanted — which degrades placement for everyone on your list.

The paradox: the worst thing you can do is nothing. Continuing to send your regular program to people who've checked out slowly poisons your sender reputation. You need to either re-engage them or remove them. Win-back campaigns force the decision.

## The win-back sequence structure

A proper win-back sequence contains three to five emails over 2–3 weeks. Each email has a distinct job.

**Email 1: The acknowledgment.** Subject line something like "Still interested?" or "We've noticed you've been away." The body acknowledges the silence without being needy. Remind them what they signed up for and why it mattered. Include a single, clear call-to-action: click this to stay subscribed. No offer yet — just test whether they care enough to click.

**Email 2: The value reminder.** Sent 4–5 days later if they didn't engage with email one. Subject line focusing on what they're missing: "You're missing our best stuff" or "Here's what you've been missing." Include 2–3 concrete examples of recent content, offers, or wins. Make it visual — screenshots, product images, testimonial quotes. The goal is to jog their memory about why they subscribed.

**Email 3: The incentive (optional).** If your business model supports it, email three offers something tangible: 15% off, free shipping, exclusive content. Subject line: "We'd love to have you back (here's 15% off)." This recovers the segment who need a commercial nudge. Skip this email if you're a pure content play or if discounting contradicts your brand positioning.

**Email 4: The preference check.** Sent 5–7 days after email three. Subject line: "Is it us, or is it the emails?" Give them options: reduce frequency, change topics, switch formats. Sometimes people aren't gone — they're just overwhelmed. A simple frequency toggle can recover subscribers who still want your content but not every day.

**Email 5: The sunset warning.** Final email, 7 days after email four. Subject line: "Last chance: we're removing inactive subscribers." Be direct: "This is the last email you'll receive unless you click below. If we don't hear from you in the next 7 days, we'll remove your address." Include a prominent "Keep me subscribed" button. No shame, no guilt — just clarity.

Anyone who doesn't respond to this sequence gets suppressed. No exceptions.

## Subject line approaches that work

Generic "We miss you" subject lines get ignored because they trigger the recipient's marketing-email filter. Effective win-back subject lines create curiosity or urgency without sounding desperate.

Curiosity-driven options:

- "Did we do something wrong?"
- "Your [account/subscription/profile] status"
- "Quick question about your subscription"
- "Is this still your email address?"

Urgency/consequence options:

- "Final notice: we're removing inactive subscribers"
- "You're about to lose access"
- "Last chance to stay on our list"

Value-focused options:

- "You're missing [specific thing they care about]"
- "Here's what you've been missing"
- "We've made some changes you should know about"

Test everything, but the subject line matters less than the from-name and preview text. If your from-name is "Newsletter" or "Marketing Team," you're already losing. Use a real person's name or your brand name, and make sure your preview text extends the subject line's hook rather than repeating it.

## When to sunset and why it matters

If someone ignores all five emails in your win-back sequence, suppress them. Not "reduce frequency" or "try again in six months" — remove them from regular sends entirely.

This is psychologically difficult. Most marketers treat list size as a vanity metric and resist any action that shrinks the number. But sending to persistently unengaged addresses actively harms your program in three ways.

First, it tanks your engagement metrics. A 10,000-person list with 3,000 dead addresses will show a 30% lower engagement rate than a 7,000-person cleaned list. Lower engagement signals to mailbox providers that your mail is less relevant, which degrades deliverability for everyone.

Second, it increases spam complaint risk. Recipients who never open your emails are more likely to hit "report spam" when they finally do see one in their inbox. One spam complaint per 1,000 sends is the informal threshold where ISPs start throttling delivery. Keep mailing to people who've checked out and you drift toward that threshold.

Third, it wastes money. Most ESPs charge by subscriber count or send volume. Every dead address on your list is a recurring cost with zero return.

The operational best practice: after the final win-back email, move unresponsive addresses to a suppression segment. Archive them — don't delete the data — but stop sending. If someone from that segment later clicks a link in a forwarded email or makes a purchase, you can reactivate them. But stop the outbound mail.

## Setting up the automation

Win-back campaigns work best as automated flows triggered by engagement criteria, not as one-off campaigns you remember to run quarterly.

In your ESP, create a segment definition: "Hasn't opened or clicked any email in [90/180] days AND hasn't made a purchase in that window." Exclude anyone already in a win-back flow (to avoid sending duplicates if they hit the threshold twice).

Build the five-email sequence as a drip campaign with the timing described above. Set the trigger to enroll people when they meet your lapsed criteria.

The last email in the sequence should have a branching workflow: if they click the "Keep me subscribed" link, move them back to your main list and exit the flow. If they reach day 21 without any engagement, move them to your suppression segment and exit.

Test the full sequence on a small sample first — 500–1,000 lapsed subscribers — before rolling it out to your entire lapsed segment. Track recovery rate (what percentage re-engaged), revenue generated, and spam complaints. If recovery rate is below 8%, revisit your messaging. If it's above 15%, you've either nailed the execution or your lapsed definition is too aggressive (catching people who aren't actually dormant).

## Mistakes that kill win-back performance

Biggest mistake: making the sequence about you instead of them. "We've made improvements" or "We've been working hard" positions the email as a company update, not a subscriber benefit. Lead with what they're missing or what they'll lose, not what you've been doing.

Second mistake: requiring too much effort to re-engage. If your "Keep me subscribed" link goes to a preference centre with twelve checkboxes, you've lost them. The click itself is the signal — don't add friction.

Third mistake: sunsetting too early. Some marketers panic and remove subscribers after a single win-back email. Give the sequence time to work. People check email sporadically, and it may take three or four attempts to catch them at a moment when they're actually paying attention.

Fourth mistake: over-incentivising. If you offer 30% off in email three, you train subscribers that ignoring you for six months earns a bigger discount. Use incentives strategically and make sure they're not larger than what you offer active subscribers.
